üí∑ Personal Allowance ‚ñº

The Personal Allowance for 2025/26 is ¬£12,570, representing the amount individuals can earn tax-free before income tax applies. This is the same for all UK taxpayers regardless of employment status.

Income above this threshold is taxed according to progressive bands. This means you only pay tax on the income that exceeds ¬£12,570.

Example:

A personal trainer earning ¬£35,000 annually would pay no tax on the first ¬£12,570, then 20% tax on the remaining ¬£22,430 (¬£35,000 - ¬£12,570), resulting in ¬£4,486 income tax liability.

Important note for higher earners: Individuals earning above ¬£100,000 experience Personal Allowance reduction at a rate of ¬£1 for every ¬£2 earned above this threshold, effectively creating a 60% marginal tax rate between ¬£100,000 and ¬£125,140. The Personal Allowance completely disappears at ¬£125,140.

üìä Income Tax Bands ‚ñº

Income above the Personal Allowance is taxed according to progressive bands:

Tax Band Income Range Tax Rate
Basic Rate ¬£12,571 to ¬£50,270 20%
Higher Rate ¬£50,271 to ¬£125,140 40%
Additional Rate Above ¬£125,140 45%

Progressive taxation means: You don't jump to a higher rate for all your income. For example, if you earn ¬£55,000, you pay 20% on the portion between ¬£12,571-¬£50,270, then 40% only on the portion between ¬£50,271-¬£55,000.

üí° Key Point:
Tax bands are progressive, meaning higher earners pay more tax overall, but only at the higher rate for income that falls into that band.

üõ°Ô∏è National Insurance Overview ‚ñº

National Insurance (NI) represents a separate contribution system funding state benefits including State Pension, unemployment support, and certain health provisions.

Unlike income tax, National Insurance operates differently for employed and self-employed individuals. It's important to understand which type applies to your situation.

Key difference: National Insurance is NOT the same as income tax. It's a separate system with different rates, thresholds, and rules depending on your employment status.
üíº National Insurance for PAYE Employees ‚ñº

Class 1 National Insurance for employees:

Income Range Employee NI Rate
¬£0 to ¬£12,570 0% (below Lower Earnings Limit)
¬£12,571 to ¬£50,270 8%
Above ¬£50,270 2%

Key points about employee NI:

  • Employees pay Class 1 National Insurance at 8% on earnings between ¬£12,570 and ¬£50,270 annually, and 2% on earnings above ¬£50,270
  • Employers also pay National Insurance on employee earnings (13.8% on earnings above ¬£9,100 annually), though this does not directly affect employee take-home pay
  • Employee National Insurance is deducted automatically through PAYE alongside income tax, appearing on payslips as "NI" or "National Insurance"
  • Unlike income tax, no personal allowance exists for National Insurance
  • Earnings below ¬£12,570 annually (the Lower Earnings Limit) do not attract NI charges, but still count toward State Pension qualification
Example:

An employee earning ¬£30,000 would pay 8% National Insurance on ¬£17,430 (¬£30,000 - ¬£12,570) = ¬£1,394.40 annually, in addition to their income tax.

üè¢ National Insurance for Self-Employed ‚ñº

Self-employed individuals pay two types of National Insurance: Class 2 and Class 4, depending on trading profits.

Annual Trading Profits National Insurance Type Rate (2025/26)
¬£0 to ¬£6,844 Class 2 ¬£3.50 per week (voluntary)
¬£6,845 to ¬£12,569 Class 2 ¬£0 per week - you don't need to pay but are counted as making Class 2 contributions to protect your NI record
¬£12,570 to ¬£50,270 Class 4 6% of profits between ¬£12,570-¬£50,270
Over ¬£50,270 Class 4 6% of profits between ¬£12,570-¬£50,270 and 2% of profits above ¬£50,270

Important distinctions:

  • Class 2 NI is a fixed weekly amount (¬£3.50/week if ¬£6,845-¬£12,569 annual profits) that contributes to State Pension eligibility
  • Class 4 NI is calculated as a percentage of profits and is charged alongside income tax through Self Assessment
  • Self-employed National Insurance is calculated through Self Assessment tax returns and paid alongside income tax, typically in January and July following the tax year end
  • Unlike employees, self-employed individuals do not have employer contributions supplementing their NI
  • This is reflected in lower overall NI rates for self-employed individuals compared to combined employee and employer contributions
Example:

A self-employed person with ¬£30,000 annual trading profits would pay Class 4 NI of 6% on ¬£17,430 (¬£30,000 - ¬£12,570) = ¬£1,045.80, with no Class 2 due as profits exceed ¬£12,570.

üí° Key Point:
Self-employed individuals should register with HMRC and complete Self Assessment tax returns to ensure their NI and tax are calculated correctly and to protect their State Pension record.
