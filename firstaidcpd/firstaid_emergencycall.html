Emergency Situations Requiring 999
‚ñº
Emergency services should be contacted for:
  • Unconscious or unresponsive individuals
  • Chest pain suggesting possible heart attack
  • Difficulty breathing not quickly resolved
  • Severe bleeding
  • Suspected spinal injury
  • Seizures lasting longer than 5 minutes or repeated seizures
  • Severe head injuries
  • Signs of stroke (sudden facial weakness, arm weakness, speech problems)
  • Any situation where you are seriously concerned about someone's condition
  • When designated first aiders or facility protocols direct emergency service contact

Time-Critical Emergencies
‚ñº
Some emergencies require immediate 999 contact because every minute matters:
  • Cardiac Arrest: Person not breathing normally or unresponsive - survival decreases 7-10% per minute without CPR/defibrillation
  • Stroke: Face-Arm-Speech-Time (FAST) - treatment within hours can prevent permanent disability
  • Severe Bleeding: Major blood loss can lead to shock and death within minutes
  • Choking: Complete airway obstruction causes unconsciousness within 2-3 minutes
  • Anaphylaxis: Severe allergic reaction can cause airway closure and death within minutes
üí° For these time-critical emergencies, call 999 immediately while beginning appropriate first aid. Don't delay the call to provide treatment.

When You're Unsure
‚ñº
If you're uncertain whether a situation requires emergency services:
  • Call 999 and describe the situation - dispatchers are trained to assess urgency
  • Consider: "Would I want someone to call for me in this situation?"
  • Trust your instincts - if something feels seriously wrong, call
  • Remember facility protocols may require 999 contact for specific situations
  • Don't worry about "wasting" emergency services time - they would rather assess than miss emergencies

Location Information
What to provide: Full facility address including building name, specific location within facility (gym floor, studio 2, changing rooms), best entry point for responders, parking information if relevant. Be as specific as possible - "Men's changing room near pool entrance" is better than just "changing rooms".
üë§
Casualty Information
What to provide: Number of casualties, brief description of what happened, whether casualty is conscious and breathing, visible injuries or symptoms, approximate age if known. Example: "One male casualty, approximately 45 years old, collapsed during exercise, unconscious but breathing, no visible injuries."
ü©π
Current Actions
What to provide: Whether anyone is providing first aid, what is currently being done. Example: "Staff member providing CPR" or "Casualty in recovery position, being monitored." This helps dispatchers know what support you need and what's already happening.
üì±
Following Dispatcher Instructions
Emergency services dispatchers may provide instructions for actions to take while awaiting responder arrival. Follow these instructions carefully and confirm understanding. Dispatchers can talk untrained persons through CPR and other interventions if necessary. They remain on the line to provide ongoing guidance.
‚è≥
Maintaining Communication
Remain on the line until told to hang up. This allows dispatchers to provide ongoing guidance and update responding crews if situations change. If you must hang up (to provide care or for safety), ensure someone knows you contacted emergency services and what instructions were provided. Have someone meet responders at the entrance.
üí° Pro Tip: Speak clearly and calmly. The dispatcher needs to understand you. Take a breath before speaking if you feel panicked. It's okay to say "I'm not sure" - they can work with that.
