Good Samaritan Principle

Understanding legal protection for emergency responders and your professional obligations

UK law generally protects individuals who provide emergency assistance in good faith, reducing concerns about legal liability that might prevent people from helping during emergencies.

1
Good Samaritan Protection
â–¼

Individuals who provide emergency assistance are generally protected from legal liability provided they:

  • Act in good faith (genuinely trying to help rather than with malicious intent)
  • Act within their training and capability
  • Exercise reasonable care given circumstances and training level
  • Do not act with gross negligence or recklessness

This legal framework encourages bystander assistance by reducing fear of lawsuits for well-intentioned helpers who make honest mistakes or cannot achieve perfect outcomes.

2
What This Means for Fitness Professionals
â–¼

Fitness professionals who recognise emergencies, follow facility protocols, activate emergency services, and provide assistance within their training level are very unlikely to face successful legal action even if outcomes are poor. Courts recognise that emergencies involve uncertainty and that helpers do their best in difficult circumstances.

However, protection does not extend to:

  • Deliberate harm or gross negligence
  • Acting significantly beyond training level
  • Ignoring obvious dangers to casualties through reckless action
  • Abandoning casualties inappropriately
3
Duty to Act
â–¼

While general members of the public have no legal duty to provide emergency assistance (though may face moral obligations), fitness professionals' duty of care to clients creates professional obligations to respond to emergencies appropriately.

Complete non-response could breach professional duty even when legal Good Samaritan protection applies.

Key Point: Your position as a fitness professional means you have higher obligations than a general bystander. You must respond appropriately to recognised emergencies.
