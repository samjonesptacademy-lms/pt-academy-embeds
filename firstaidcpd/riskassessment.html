The Risk Assessment Process
Risk assessment follows a systematic 4-step approach to identify and control hazards
1Hazard Identification
Recognise anything with potential to cause harm: equipment (weights, machines, functional tools), activities (high-intensity training, martial arts, aquatics), environmental factors (wet areas, steps, doors), and population considerations (elderly, health conditions, children).
2Risk Evaluation
Assess likelihood of harm occurring and potential severity. Consider frequency of exposure, number of people exposed, existing controls, and historical incident data.
3Control Measures
Introduce measures to eliminate hazards or reduce risks: engineering controls (equipment design, modifications), administrative controls (policies, procedures, training, supervision), and PPE where appropriate.
4Regular Review
Reassess risks periodically or following incidents, near-misses, or significant changes to activities, equipment, facilities, or populations.

Hazard: Free Weight Dropping
Risk: Foot/limb injuries, damaged floors
Controls: Designated free weight areas, proper lifting technique instruction, appropriate flooring, rules prohibiting unnecessary weight dropping
Hazard: Cardiovascular Equipment
Risk: Falls, entanglement, cardiac events
Controls: Proper equipment maintenance, emergency stop mechanisms, instruction on equipment use, supervision during initial use, medical screening for high-risk individuals
Hazard: Wet Changing Room Floors
Risk: Slips and falls
Controls: Non-slip flooring, regular cleaning and drying, adequate drainage, wet floor signage, appropriate footwear policies

Scenario: Group HIIT Class
You're running a high-intensity interval training class with 20 participants of mixed abilities. The class involves burpees, box jumps, kettlebell swings, and sprint intervals. Some participants are new to HIIT training. The studio has rubber flooring and equipment is stored along the walls.
High-impact movements (burpees, box jumps)
Mixed ability levels in class
Kettlebell swings (improper technique/dropping)
Box jump equipment (falls, shin injuries)
High cardiovascular demands
Crowding with 20 participants
Fatigue affecting form and coordination
Muscle strains and joint sprains
Falls and impact injuries
Overexertion and exhaustion
Cardiac events in susceptible individuals
Collisions between participants
Equipment-related injuries (dropped weights, shin strikes)
Provide proper demonstration and technique instruction
Offer exercise modifications for different fitness levels
Ensure adequate spacing between participants
Comprehensive warm-up and cool-down
Active supervision and correction during exercises
Pre-class health screening for new participants
Equipment safety checks before class

Scenario: Pool Area Management
Your gym has a swimming pool used for aqua aerobics classes, lane swimming, and general recreation. The pool area includes wet changing rooms, a poolside with loungers, and shower facilities. Members range from young adults to elderly users, and some bring children.
Drowning risk
Wet and slippery surfaces
Pool chemicals (chlorine exposure)
Varying pool depths
Unsupervised children
Elderly users with mobility issues
Running near pool edge
Pool equipment and loungers (trip hazards)
Drowning or near-drowning incidents
Slips, trips and falls
Head injuries from falls on hard surfaces
Chemical irritation (eyes, skin, respiratory)
Diving injuries in shallow water
Child-specific accidents and injuries
Qualified lifeguard supervision during operating hours
Non-slip flooring throughout wet areas
Clear depth markers and signage
Child supervision policy and age restrictions
Emergency rescue equipment (reach poles, flotation devices)
Regular cleaning and drying of wet areas
Proper pool chemical management and testing
Clear pool rules (no running, no diving in shallow areas)
